# Exercise: Have the AI grill you

Bring something you have already made, written, or collected (a draft,
a project plan, a dataset, a half finished idea) and use the AI as a
sceptical interviewer. The output of this exercise is not a new
artefact. It is a sharper understanding of your own work.

## At a glance

- **Bring**: anything of yours. A draft email, a project proposal, an exhibition concept, a CSV you have been staring at, a wiki page, a job description, a research question.
- **Deliverable**: a list of questions, weaknesses, or blind spots the AI flagged, plus your answers to the ones that matter.
- **Time**: 15 to 30 minutes.
- **AI tool**: any chat assistant (Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot).

## Why this exercise exists

Most people use AI to generate things. This exercise uses it the other
way around: as a sparring partner that pushes back on what you already
have. It is the fastest way to find the gaps in your own thinking, and
it costs nothing but a few messages.

## The steps

### 1. Pick what you want grilled

One thing, not five. A specific draft, dataset, or idea. The more
concrete, the better the grilling.

### 2. Paste it in

Drop the full thing into the chat. If it is a dataset, paste the first
20 to 50 rows so the AI can see the shape.

### 3. Ask to be grilled

Use a prompt like one of these. Pick the angle that fits what you brought.

For a piece of writing or a plan:

> You are a sceptical senior colleague. Read the draft below and find
> the five weakest points. For each, say what is wrong and what a
> reader would ask. Do not soften your feedback. Do not rewrite
> anything yet.
>
> [paste your draft]

For a dataset:

> Below is a sample of a dataset I am working with. Act as a data
> reviewer. List ten things that look inconsistent, suspicious, or
> likely to cause problems downstream. For each, say why it matters
> and what I should check.
>
> [paste 20 to 50 rows]

For a project idea:

> Below is a one paragraph description of a project I am planning.
> Interview me. Ask the ten questions a sceptical funder or manager
> would ask before approving it. Number them. Do not answer them yourself.
>
> [paste the description]

### 4. Read the response slowly

Some of the points will land. Some will miss. That is fine. The signal
is in the ones that make you say "oh, yes, I had not thought about that".

### 5. Answer the questions that matter

Pick the three or four points that actually changed your mind or
exposed a gap. Write your answer next to each one. This is the real
output of the exercise.

### 6. Push back

If the AI was too gentle, say so:

> That was too polite. Be harsher. Tell me what is actually wrong, not
> what is mildly imperfect.

If it invented criticism that does not apply, push back too:

> Point 3 is not a real problem because [reason]. Drop it and find a
> better one.

The goal is not to agree with the AI. It is to use it to think harder.

## Variations

- **Roast my CV**, **roast my abstract**, **roast my exhibition
  proposal**. Same recipe, different artefact.
- **Have it interview you as a journalist.** Useful when you are about
  to present something publicly.
- **Have it interview you as a future user.** Useful for tools,
  guides, or anything someone else will have to follow.

## Tips

- The first response is rarely the most useful one. Push for a second pass.
- The AI is not a domain expert. Treat its questions as prompts for
  your own thinking, not as verdicts.
- Keep the questions that landed. They are reusable: next time you
  start a similar project, you can grill yourself with them up front.

## Going further

- Have the AI grill the same thing in three different roles (sceptical
  funder, friendly colleague, hostile reviewer). Compare which one
  surfaced what.
- Hand the AI your own answers from step 5 and ask it to grill *those*.
- Read [How to prompt](../prompts/how-to-prompt.md), in particular the
  *Roast me* and *Suggest improvements as a numbered list* patterns.
  This exercise is those patterns turned into a workshop step.
